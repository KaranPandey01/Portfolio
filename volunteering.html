Teaching beyond the terminal

Outside of systems and code, I believe in giving back — especially in STEM education access for underprivileged children. Volunteering at Sakhi Sahiyog gave me a different kind of engineering challenge: how do you make learning stick for kids who've never had consistent access to it?

What I did
  • Helped underprivileged children by educating them in key subjects of STEM — making complex concepts accessible and engaging for kids with limited prior exposure
  • Applied award-based learning methodology — using positive reinforcement and milestone rewards to stimulate continued interest in learning, turning curiosity into habit
  • Used multiple teaching methods adapted to individual learning styles — visual aids, interactive demonstrations, and hands-on problem-solving to accommodate different paces
  • Taught through one-on-one interaction to give each child focused attention, identify where they were struggling, and personalise explanations in real time

"The best engineers are also great teachers — they can break down complexity until it's obvious. Volunteering forced me to do exactly that, for an audience that had no context and no patience for jargon. It made me a sharper communicator."

— Karan Pandey, on the experience

What it taught me

🗣️
Clear communication
Explaining concepts to kids with no background forces radical clarity. That skill translates directly to writing clean documentation, readable code, and effective PR reviews.
🎯
Adaptive problem-solving
When one teaching method doesn't work, you iterate fast. The same mindset applies in debugging — try a different angle, check your assumptions, and adapt.
🌱
Patience & persistence
Progress isn't always linear. Teaching reinforced that consistent effort over time — even in small sessions — creates lasting change. True of learning, systems, and people.
